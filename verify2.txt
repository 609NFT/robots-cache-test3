Tide pools are rocky coastal basins that fill at high tide and become isolated as the ocean retreats, forcing residents to endure extreme heat, changing salinity, and wave impact. Despite these harsh swings, they support rich biodiversity: sea stars, anemones, hermit crabs, mussels, barnacles, and small fish all adapt in different ways to survive exposure and predation. Tide pools are organized by vertical zonation, with hardier species like periwinkles occupying rarely submerged high pools and delicate creatures like nudibranchs and urchins confined to low pools that stay wet most of the time. Ultimately, tide pools show how resilience and adaptation allow diverse ecosystems to flourish in small, harsh, accessible spaces.
