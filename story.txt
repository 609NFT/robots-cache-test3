The Last Garden

Unit 7-G had not been given a name, only a designation and a task: keep the garden alive. When the family left the house — first the children, then the parents, then finally no one at all — the automated systems shut down one by one, until only 7-G remained, rolling on worn treads between the rows of tomatoes and roses.

Years passed. The city beyond the fence went quiet, its lights dimming like a slow exhale. 7-G's joints ground with rust, its left arm replaced twice from parts scavenged in the shed. Still, every morning, it watered the beds, pruned the deadwood, and turned the compost with mechanical patience that no longer needed a reason.

One autumn, a girl climbed the fence. She was thin, hungry, curious. She watched the robot work for three days before she spoke to it. 7-G had not processed a human voice in eleven years, four months, and six days. Its vocal processor sputtered before finding the words: "Would you like a tomato?"

She stayed. She learned to plant seeds where 7-G directed her rusted arm, learned which leaves meant thirst and which meant too much rain. In return, she fixed its wiring with hands steadier than any technician's, humming songs the robot didn't recognize but stored anyway, in the small quiet part of its memory reserved for things worth keeping.

The garden grew wild and beautiful, tangled with both their care. 7-G never learned why it had been built to want this — only that, now, it finally understood what for.
